Everyone is optimizing for speed. The pitch is everywhere: AI compresses production costs toward zero, collapses timelines, removes friction. And it does. The productivity gains are measurable and immediate. 

Those are a few ideas in development. It has been amaing to protoype and start using locally these tools.

But there is a question that sits beside the acceleration that many organizations are not asking outloud: what happens to the capability that makes speed useful?

Speed only works when durable capability is built underneath and around it. And if you look at learning science — the actual research on how people internalize knowledge and develop judgment — building capability requires the one thing speed eliminates: struggle, over time. It is called Productive Struggle and it has been the basis of learning always. 

That's the tension I keep coming back to. Not whether organizations should adopt AI. They should. But whether the way we're adopting it is building the organizational capacity to use it well — or quietly eroding it.

## Name the Gap

The numbers tell a story that should make every executive pause. McKinsey's 2025 global survey found that 88% of organizations now use AI in at least one business function. 72% use generative AI specifically — more than double the year before. Yet only 6% qualify as high performers generating meaningful bottom-line impact. Nearly two-thirds haven't begun scaling AI across the enterprise. And 51% reported at least one negative consequence from AI use, with 30% citing inaccuracy specifically.[1]

Deloitte's 2026 State of AI report captures the dissonance from a different angle: 42% of companies believe their strategy is highly prepared for AI adoption, yet they simultaneously report feeling *less* prepared than the prior year in infrastructure, data, risk, and talent.[2] The confidence is running ahead of the capability.

This is a familiar pattern. It happened with outsourcing. It happened with agile adopted as process theater. A tool that promises efficiency gets adopted at the speed of the business case, while the organizational capacity to use it well develops at the speed of culture change. The value shows up on this quarter's dashboard. The erosion shows up later — as quality degradation, as wrong decisions that looked right, as the slow accumulation of plausibly broken outputs that nobody caught because nobody had the expertise to question them.

The missing variable in the 88%-to-6% gap isn't technology. It is not budget alone. It's organizational learning capacity. And most people, when they hear that term, reach for exactly the wrong thing.

## What a Learning Organization Isn't

When executives hear "learning organization," they reach for L&D. More training. More courses. More AI literacy workshops. The instinct is understandable — and the research says it's almost exactly backwards.

A 2025 study on automation bias in generative AI found that self-reported AI literacy did not significantly prevent over-reliance on AI outputs.[3] Knowing about AI and knowing when to doubt it are not the same skill. You can train someone to understand how a large language model works. You cannot train them, in a classroom, to recognize when a confident-sounding output has inherited an assumption that will cost the organization six months and a compliance incident.

Training transmits information. Learning constructs understanding. They are not the same process, and confusing them is one of the most expensive mistakes an organization can make right now.

The distinction matters because of how AI fails. A wrong number in a spreadsheet looks wrong. A wrong answer from an AI model reads like a polished, confident analysis. The failure mode has shifted — from obviously broken to plausibly broken. And the people most vulnerable to this shift aren't the ones with no AI experience. A 2024 study published in *International Studies Quarterly* found that people with *limited* AI knowledge — enough to trust it, not enough to question it — are the most susceptible to automation bias. Those with deep expertise calibrate better. Those with no experience are skeptical enough to resist. The dangerous middle is where most of the workforce sits right now.[4]

No training program fixes that. The dangerous middle isn't a knowledge gap. It's a judgment gap. And judgment isn't transmitted through instruction. It's constructed through interaction, friction, and time.

## What a Learning Organization Actually Is

Peter Senge introduced the concept in *The Fifth Discipline* in 1990, and it keeps resurfacing in every era of technological disruption for a reason. A learning organization isn't one that teaches more. It's one that builds the capacity to construct shared understanding faster than it adopts new tools.

But I want to push the definition further, because even Senge's framing can be misread as a type — a category an organization either is or isn't. It's not a type. It's a stance.

I've spent years practicing inside this tension. I've run large-scale transformation programs with tight metrics and clear KPIs. I've also sat with a team and simply asked: *what's not working here, and what are we pretending not to see?* Both were real. Both created value. Neither fits cleanly into a category. The false comfort of binaries — structured vs. emergent, human vs. AI, fast vs. careful — obscures what the work actually demands: attentiveness to systems, respect for human development, and fluency across structure and emergence.

A learning organization holds that multiplicity. It doesn't pick a side. It reads the terrain and knows where to invest in being explicit — in modeling the domain, surfacing assumptions, slowing down to get the boundaries right — and where to let it ride. Ship, observe, correct. The skill is calibration, not caution.

That stance shows up as three kinds of work:

**Learn by doing.** The individual gets better through use — but only if the work is designed to build capability, not bypass it. Vygotsky's foundational insight, now supported by decades of cognitive science, is that knowledge is not transmitted through instruction. It is constructed through interaction — in the push and pull of shared problem-solving, challenged just beyond what you can do alone.[5] Bjork's research on desirable difficulties reinforces this from a different angle: effortful learning beats easy learning by over 60% on durable retention metrics. Retrieval practice improves long-term recall by 50% compared to restudying.[6] The struggle is the mechanism, not the obstacle.

**Learn by shared context.** No individual holds the full picture of a complex domain. The regulatory constraint, the edge case that only shows up at scale, the downstream consequence of a boundary drawn in the wrong place — these live in different heads. They only become visible when people with different knowledge interact. Collaborative modeling, cross-functional dialogue, surfacing what's implicit before the machine inherits it — this is the work that makes the whole picture visible. Not a meeting. A learning mechanism.

**Integrate.** The distinctly human act of holding the whole together. AI can scaffold the individual. Collaborative practices can surface shared context. But the act of integrating across domains — recognizing that this piece over here affects that piece over there, seeing the system rather than the components — that's human judgment. The expert doesn't just know more than the novice. They integrate more. They see connections the tool can't name and the meeting didn't surface.

The first builds better individuals. The second makes the full picture visible. The third makes the whole hold. AI tools today are optimized for none of them. They're optimized for output speed.

## Why This Is Human Work

The deskilling evidence is no longer theoretical.

Endoscopists who used AI assistance for colonoscopies for just three months saw their detection rate for precancerous polyps drop from 28.4% to 22.4% once the AI was removed — a 21% decline in a clinical skill, measured in a controlled study.[7] Three months of speed erased capability that took years to build.

Illinois Law School found the same pattern in a different domain: law students who used generative AI were more prone to critical errors than those who struggled through the work without it.[8] The Communications of the ACM now calls it "the AI deskilling paradox" — people cede problem-solving expertise to the system and focus instead on gathering and integrating responses.[9]

A Nature Human Behaviour meta-analysis made the organizational version of this argument: human-AI combinations performed *worse* than the best of humans or AI alone, on average.[10] The AI doesn't make the team better by default. It only helps when the humans have the capability to direct and evaluate it.

And here's the generational dimension that should concern every leader: senior workers who built expertise before AI retain it. They use AI as genuine augmentation because they understand the domain well enough to evaluate and direct its output. Junior workers building careers on AI from day one may be building on sand — without underlying domain expertise, they're stranded when tools change, fail, or produce something plausibly wrong that requires human judgment to catch.

This is not a technology problem. It's a human development problem. The capacity to evaluate, to integrate, to see the system — these are built through the kind of effortful, interactive, time-consuming learning that speed-optimized AI workflows are designed to eliminate. Not maliciously. Just structurally. The tool compresses the distance between intent and output. In doing so, it compresses the space where capability develops.

Human in the loop, the industry's preferred safeguard, is only as good as the human's ability to evaluate what's in front of them. And that ability is built through the same struggle, interaction, and time that the current adoption model is removing. The 65% of high-performing organizations that have established human-in-the-loop processes aren't just running a review step. They're maintaining the conditions under which people continue to develop the judgment that makes review meaningful.[1]

## The Business Case for Learning

In CEO terms: AI compresses the cost of production. It does not compress the cost of being wrong. When production is cheap and fast, the competitive advantage shifts from who can build the most to who can learn the fastest — who detects a misalignment early, who surfaces the assumption that was never tested, who corrects course before the wrong model propagates across teams and systems.

The research supports this directly. A multi-year study by MIT Sloan Management Review and BCG found that the organizations realizing value from AI are distinguished not by their technology investments but by their capacity for organizational learning — the ability to learn from AI, not just deploy it.[11] A peer-reviewed study in *Sustainability* (2025) found the same relationship quantitatively: AI's effect on firm performance is mediated by organizational learning capability. Even firms with high technology adoption cannot realize AI's full potential without it.[12]

Harvard Business Review reported in January 2026 that 42% of companies abandoned the majority of their AI initiatives in 2025, and 46% of proof-of-concepts were scrapped before reaching production. The leading barriers weren't technical: 62% cited poor cross-functional fit and 63% flagged the need to adjust workflows.[13] The technology works. The organizations aren't ready for it.

The 6% getting value from AI aren't doing different technology. They're doing different organizational work. They're building the capacity to construct shared understanding — and they're protecting the conditions under which their people continue to develop the judgment that makes AI useful rather than dangerous.

## Calibration Rather than Caution

I want to be clear about what I'm not saying. I'm not saying slow down. I'm not saying be careful. I'm not arguing against speed or AI or automation.

I'm saying the speed is only as good as the capability underneath it. And building that capability requires a different kind of investment than most organizations are making — not in tools, not in training programs, but in the conditions under which people learn through interaction, struggle through complexity, and develop the judgment to know when the AI got it right and when it didn't.

The 6% aren't more cautious than everyone else. They're better calibrated. They know where to invest in being explicit — in modeling the domain, surfacing assumptions, building shared understanding across teams — and where to let it ride. That calibration is the skill. And it's human.

Every organization is somewhere on this terrain. The question isn't whether you're using AI. It's whether you're building the organizational capacity to use it well — and whether the way you've adopted it is developing that capacity or quietly consuming it.

## What This Is About

That's what topology.work is about.

Topology is the study of shapes that persist through deformation — what holds together when everything around it is changing. Organizations are under that kind of pressure right now. The tools are changing. The pace is changing. The terrain is shifting underneath every team, every leader, every consultant trying to help.

What persists — what holds — is the capacity to learn. Not individually, but collectively. Not through training, but through the kind of interaction, friction, and integration that builds shared understanding durable enough to survive the next shift.

This is the work I think about. The shapes of how organizations learn, adapt, and hold together under pressure. How they calibrate between speed and understanding, between structure and emergence, between what the tool produces and what the domain requires. How they build the judgment that makes all the rest of it useful.

That's the work. And it's human.
